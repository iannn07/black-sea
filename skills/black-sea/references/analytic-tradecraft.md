# Analytic Tradecraft

The engine room. These are real, publicly documented structured analytic techniques (Heuer &
Pherson; ICD 203; the Admiralty/NATO source-rating system). Use them so judgments are testable, not
vibes.

## 1. Three labels, never blurred

- **FACT** — directly sourced and verifiable. Cite it (→ Evidence Register).
- **ASSESSMENT** — your reasoned judgment *from* facts. Always carries a confidence grade.
- **ASSUMPTION** — an unverified premise you're carrying to proceed. Log it; flag if load-bearing.

If a sentence mixes them ("the CFO clearly moved the money offshore"), split it: what's sourced, what
you infer, what you're assuming.

## 2. Source reliability — Admiralty Code

Grade every source on two independent axes. Put the code in the Evidence Register.

**Reliability of the source (A–F):**
A completely reliable · B usually reliable · C fairly reliable · D not usually reliable ·
E unreliable · F cannot be judged.

**Credibility of the information (1–6):**
1 confirmed by other sources · 2 probably true · 3 possibly true · 4 doubtful · 5 improbable ·
6 cannot be judged.

A primary filing from an official registry might be **A1**. An anonymous forum post corroborated
nowhere is **E5/F6**. Never let a low-grade source silently become a high-confidence finding.

Corroboration rule: independent sources that don't share an origin. Ten sites republishing one press
release is **one** source, not ten.

## 3. Confidence & words of estimative probability (ICD 203 style)

Rate each key judgment **High / Moderate / Low** confidence, and state *why* (quality + corroboration
+ gaps). Keep likelihood language and confidence separate — they're different axes.

Likelihood ladder (use consistently, avoid vague "possibly"):
almost no chance · very unlikely · unlikely · roughly even chance · likely · very likely · almost certain.

Example: "Acme **likely** overstated Q3 revenue (**Moderate** confidence) — two independent
indicators, but no access to the underlying ledgers."

## 4. Key Assumptions Check

Before finalizing, list every assumption the analysis rests on. For each: *How confident am I? What
happens to the case if it's wrong? What would tell me it's wrong?* Assumptions that would flip the
conclusion get promoted to explicit risks in the dossier.

## 5. Analysis of Competing Hypotheses (ACH)

The antidote to confirmation bias. For any contested conclusion:

1. Enumerate the **plausible hypotheses** (at least 2 — include the "innocent explanation").
2. List the evidence and each key assumption.
3. For each piece of evidence, ask which hypotheses it's **consistent / inconsistent** with.
4. **Focus on disconfirmation.** The strongest hypothesis is the one with the least evidence against
   it, not the most for it. A fact consistent with every hypothesis has no diagnostic value — drop it.
5. Report the surviving hypothesis, the runners-up, and the specific evidence that would change the
   ranking.

## 6. Quality-of-information & bias checks

- **Deception check:** could this source *want* me to believe this? (planted filings, astroturf, SEO).
- **Anchoring:** am I over-weighting the first thing I found?
- **Vividness:** am I over-weighting a dramatic single data point over dull aggregates?
- **Devil's advocate:** actively argue the opposite of your leading judgment for one pass. If it
  holds up too easily, your confidence is too high.

## 7. Estimating vs. asserting

When you don't have a number, say so and bound it ("between X and Y, based on Z") rather than
inventing precision. A defensible range beats a fabricated point every time.
